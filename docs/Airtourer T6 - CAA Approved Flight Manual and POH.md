## 1.2 General Description

### 1.2.1 General Description of the Aircraft

The Airtourer T6 aircraft is a low wing, fixed tricycle undercarriage, single engine, two seat light aircraft. The fuselage consists basically of two main sections — the rear fuselage and the front fuselage — joined together to form a single unit. The front fuselage extends from the firewall to the rear of the cockpit and incorporates the cockpit, windshield and sliding canopy. The rear fuselage extends from the rear of the cockpit to the tail cone. The fuselage is of conventional rivetted aluminium alloy sheet metal construction.

### 1.2.2 Engine & Propeller

A four cylinder, horizontally opposed, air cooled, normally aspirated Textron Lycoming O-320 engine is installed, rated at 150 or 160 BHP. The engine drives a two blade Hartzell constant speed propeller.

### 1.2.3 Seating Capacity

Two seats are provided, side by side, and enclosed by a fixed windscreen and a sliding perspex canopy.

### 1.2.4 Wing

The wing is of single-spar construction, secured to the fuselage by means of fore and aft pins passing through the main spar from the spar attachment frame below the seats, and by spanwise bolts passing through the fuselage lower longerons and the angle brackets on the rear spar. Full span flapperon/ailerons are fitted and operated in conjunction with each other as ailerons with stick side movement, as flaps by means of the flap lever, with differential movement between flaps and ailerons.

### 1.2.5 Undercarriage

Tricycle type undercarriage is fitted. Nose-wheel steering is achieved by direct linkage with rudder pedals. The main undercarriage is attached to the wing ribs and consists of two leaf spring type shock absorbing legs of fail/safe design and low pressure wheels. The nose-wheel leg is a coil spring automotive hydraulic shock absorber assembly fitted with a low pressure wheel. Dunlop hydraulic disc brakes are fitted, and are operated by individual hand pull levers within easy reach of the pilot. A centrally mounted park brake selector can be used by either pilot to maintain brake pressure after individual levers have been released.

### 1.2.6 Empennage

The empennage consists of a single fin and aero-dynamically balanced rudder mounted wholly above the fuselage, and a single piece tailplane with full span elevator.

### 1.2.7 Fuel System

The fuel system consists of a rubber bag fuel tank located in the wing centre section beneath the pilots seats. A filler neck is provided on the starboard side of the fuselage above the wing. The tank is vented via a small air scoop located mid way down the starboard side of the rear fuselage. A sediment drain is located underneath the fuselage on the port side just near the inboard end of the port flapperon. The fuel tank has a total capacity of 131.8 litres.

| Symbol | Definition |

### 1.4.3 Meteorological Terminology

**ISA** — *International Standard Atmosphere* in which the air is a dry perfect gas; temperature at sea level is 15°C; pressure at sea level is 1013.25 hPa; temperature gradient from sea level to 36,089 feet is -1.98°C per 1000 feet.

**OAT** — *Outside Air Temperature:* the outside free air static temperature.

**Airfield Pressure Height** — The height registered at the surface of an aerodrome by an altimeter with the pressure sub-scale set to 1013 hPa.

**Pressure Altitude** — Altitude measured from standard sea-level pressure (1013 hPa) by an altimeter corrected for position and instrument error.

**Wind** — The wind velocities to be used as variables on aircraft performance are to be understood as the headwind or tailwind components of the reported winds.

### 1.4.4 Power Terminology

**Take-Off Power** — Maximum power permissible for take-off.  
**Maximum Continuous Power** — Maximum power that is allowed to be used continuously during flight.  
**Maximum Climb Power** — Maximum power permissible during climb.  
**Maximum Cruise Power** — Maximum power permissible during cruise.

### 1.4.5 Engine Controls and Instruments

**Throttle Lever** — The lever which the pilot uses to control the engine power.  
**Mixture Control** — The control that is used to vary the fuel/air ratio available to the engine.  
**Tachometer** — The instrument that indicates the engine RPM.

### 1.4.6 Aircraft Performance and Flight Planning Terminology

**Climb Gradient** — The ratio of the change in height during a climb, to the horizontal distance travelled.  
**Maximum Crosswind Component** — The maximum allowable crosswind component for take-off and landing.

### 1.4.7 Weight and Balance Terminology

**Reference Datum** — An imaginary vertical plane from which all horizontal distances are measured for balance purposes.  
**Station** — A location along the aircraft fuselage given as a distance from the reference datum.  
**Arm** — The horizontal distance from the reference datum to the centre of gravity (CoG) of an item.  
**Moment** — The product of the weight of an item multiplied by its arm.  
**Centre of Gravity (CoG)** — The point at which an aircraft would balance if suspended. The distance from the CoG to the reference datum can be found by dividing the total moment by the total weight of the aircraft.  
**CoG Arm** — The arm obtained by adding the aircraft's individual moments and dividing the sum by the total weight.  
**CoG Limits** — The extreme centre of gravity locations within which the aircraft must be operated at a given weight.  
**Useable Fuel** — Fuel available that may be used in flight.  
**Unusable Fuel** — Fuel remaining in the aircraft that cannot be safely used in flight.  
**Empty Weight** — Weight of aircraft with unusable fuel and full oil.  
**Useful Load** — Difference between take-off weight and empty weight.  
**Maximum Take-Off Weight** — Maximum weight approved for take-off.  
**Maximum Landing Weight** — Maximum weight approved for the landing.

# SECTION 3 — EMERGENCY PROCEDURES

## 3.1 General

Section 3 of this Flight Manual describes the procedures to be adopted in the event of an emergency or abnormal situation occurring in the Airtourer T6 aircraft.

The procedures are arranged in the sequence considered to be the most desirable in the majority of cases. Steps should be performed in the order listed unless good reasons for deviation exist.

It should be remembered however, that all conceivable eventualities cannot be foreseen by the manufacturer. Particular circumstances such as multiple or unanticipated emergencies, adverse weather etc. may require modification to these procedures. A thorough knowledge of the aircraft and it's systems is essential to analyse the situation correctly and determine the best course of action in any particular circumstance.

*The following basic rules apply to all aircraft emergencies:*
1. Maintain Aircraft Control.
2. Analyse the situation and take appropriate action.
3. Land as soon as practicable.

## 4.4 Amplified Procedures

### 4.4.1 Taxying

The wide track tricycle undercarriage, coupled with the nose wheel steering and efficient braking system allows high wind conditions to be handled easily. The aircraft has been demonstrated as safe to taxi in winds up to 30 knots. When taxying in strong winds additional care should be taken, particularly in quartering tail wind conditions. Appropriate elevator and aileron control positions are essential when taxying in strong tail wind conditions.

Taxying over loose gravel or stones should be done at low engine RPM to minimise propeller damage.

### 4.4.2 Run-Up

Full throttle run-ups over loose gravel are especially harmful to the propeller and should be avoided. When take-offs must be made from a gravel surface, it is very important that the throttle be advanced slowly and a rolling start take-off technique be used to minimise propeller damage.

Prior to take-off from high elevation airfields, the mixture should be leaned just enough to ensure smooth engine operation in a full throttle static runup.

### 4.4.3 Normal Take-Off

Select 8° of flap, align the aircraft carefully with the take-off path, release the brakes and then open the throttle slowly to the fully open position.

> **NOTE:** *It is important to check full throttle engine performance early in the take-off run. Any sign of rough engine operation or sluggish acceleration is good cause for discontinuing the take off and conducting a full power runup to confirm normal engine operation prior to the next take-off attempt.*

Keep straight by use of the rudder. There is a slight tendency to swing to the left if the throttle is opened too quickly. Weight should be eased off the nose-wheel at 45–50 KIAS.

The aircraft should be flown off the ground at approximately 60 KIAS. Allow the airspeed to build up to 70 KIAS before climbing. At approximately 200 feet flaps may be raised, and airspeed increased to the recommended climb speed of 80 KIAS.

### 4.4.4 Short Field Take-Off

A short field take-off technique **must be used in order to achieve the take-off distances specified in Section 5.** The 8° of flap must be used. The shortest take-off run will be obtained if the aircraft is held against the brakes until full power has been applied.

If obstacle clearance is a consideration a speed of 65 KIAS (which gives the best angle of climb) should be maintained until clear of all obstacles. Speed should then be increased to recommended climb speed of 80 KIAS after raising flap.

> **CAUTION:** *Excessive rotation on take-off may cause the rear tail skid to scrape the ground. Normally any scraping which may occur is of a minor nature but if a severe scrape occurs, the aircraft should be inspected and certified as airworthy before further flight.*

### 4.4.5 Cross Wind Take-Off

Nosewheel steering can be used effectively during crosswind take-offs and it is advantageous to keep some weight on the nosewheel during initial acceleration until rudder control is fully obtained. The following procedure should be applied:

1. Open the throttle slowly, keeping straight with combined use of rudder and nosewheel steering and by holding the control column into wind.
2. Hold the aircraft on the ground until a speed of 55 KIAS is reached, then unstick with a positive movement.
3. Adjust the airspeed to 70 KIAS (with flap) laying off the effect of drift by yawing the nose into wind as required.

> **NOTE:** *The maximum allowable crosswind component is 20 knots.*

### 4.4.6 Climbing

The speed for maximum rate of climb is 80 KIAS flaps up. However a climb speed of 85 KIAS provides more positive control with a lower nose attitude, better visibility and better engine cooling, and may be used when maximum climb performance is not required.

Climbing may be safely accomplished at full throttle and 2700 RPM. Nevertheless a lower power setting may be used if desired, usually not below 25″Hg/2500 RPM. Rich mixture should always be used for climbing unless leaning is necessary to eliminate rough running due to an over richness at altitude, normally occurring not below 5000 feet.

### 4.4.7 General Flying

**Flying Controls** — The controls are well harmonised and the aircraft is easy and pleasant to fly. The controls remain light and effective throughout the speed range, except that the ailerons tend to become heavier as V_NE is approached.

**Changes of Trim:**
- Flaps Down: Slightly Nose Down
- Flaps Up: Slightly Nose Up

Changes of power and speed promote very slight changes in directional trim.

**Stability** — The aircraft is easy to trim under all conditions of flight, and holds its trimmed speed well.

### 4.4.8 Flying at Reduced Airspeeds

Reduce speed to 70 KIAS and lower 8° of flap, then adjust power to maintain 65–70 KIAS. Stalling speed under these conditions is approximately 53 KIAS.

### 4.4.9 Carburettor Icing

Carburettor icing is indicated by rough running and/or loss of power. To remedy, select HOT air with the carburettor heat control until icing has cleared, then re-select COLD air. If icing does not clear shortly after the selection of HOT, manipulation of the throttle may assist.

*Do not use partial heat.* Use FULL HOT or FULL COLD only.

> **CAUTION:** *Carburettor heat will increase fuel consumption and reduce maximum available engine power.*

Avoid flying close to or underneath well developed cumulus/cumulonimbus clouds.

### 4.4.10 Mixture Control

The mixture control should be set to fully rich for starting, ground running, take-off, climb and landing. Changes in altitude will require corresponding adjustments of the mixture control, normally not below 5000 feet. It should always be returned to the fully rich position before commencing a dive and/or acrobatics.

The engine should never be allowed to run for any length of time at reduced power where the reduction in power is obtained by use of the mixture control. An over weak mixture will lead to the engine over-heating, detonation and engine damage.

### 4.4.11 Vapour Lock

When operating in very hot weather there is a possibility of vapour forming in the fuel suction lines. Idling on the ground should, therefore, be reduced to the minimum, and RPM maintained at a higher figure than that normally recommended. For the same reason flight above 10,000 feet is not recommended for extended periods. Power loss due to vapour formation may be restored by turning the electric fuel pump on, and/or descending to a lower altitude.

### 4.4.12 Normal Cruising

**Maximum Cruise Speed** — The maximum cruise speed is 125 KIAS at Full throttle/2700 RPM. The mixture should be full rich when cruising at full power.

**Normal Cruise** — A normal cruise power setting of 24″Hg/2400 RPM will give a cruise speed of approximately 120 KIAS. Below 5000 ft the mixture may be left full rich, but above 5000 ft the mixture should always be correctly leaned.

*Leaning Procedure:*
1. Lean the mixture by slowly pulling the mixture control back towards the lean position until slight rough running occurs.
2. Slowly move the mixture control back towards rich until smooth running is just restored.

**Flying for Range** — The airspeed required to achieve maximum range is 110 KIAS:
1. Reduce RPM to 2300 and adjust the throttle to obtain the recommended speed. The manifold pressure will be approximately 23″Hg.
2. Lean the mixture by slowly pulling the mixture control back towards the lean position until slight rough running occurs.
3. Slowly move the mixture control back towards rich until smooth running is just restored.

**Flying for Endurance** — The airspeed required to achieve maximum endurance is 95 KIAS:
1. Reduce RPM to 2100 and adjust the throttle to obtain the recommended speed.
2. Lean the mixture by slowly pulling the mixture control back towards the lean position until slight rough running occurs.
3. Slowly move the mixture control back towards rich until smooth running is just restored.

### 4.4.13 Gliding

The best gliding angle (greatest gliding distance for any given height loss) will be achieved with flaps UP at 85 KIAS. The gliding angle will be improved slightly with the propeller pitch set to COARSE. The gliding angle with flaps extended will be slightly worse than with the flaps UP.

### 4.4.14 Side Slipping

Side slipping may be used to increase drag such as may be desirable during a forced landing approach. Side slips may be carried out with flaps extended.

### 4.4.16 Stalling

*Before practising stalling complete the Pre-Stalling and Acrobatics checks.*

Approximate stalling speeds at maximum AUW @ 862 kg:

| Configuration | Stall Speed |
|---|---|
| Power Off, flaps up | 55 KIAS |
| Power Off, flaps full down | 49 KIAS |
| Power On, under typical approach conditions with full flap | 45 KIAS |

Stall warning in all cases consists of aural warning 5–10 knots before the stall occurs. There is no obvious pre-stall airframe buffet.

With power off at the stall, the nose drops gently, with a slight tendency for the nose to oscillate in pitch. With power on, there is a well defined tendency for a wing to drop. Stalling speeds are reduced by about two to three knots with one pilot only in the aircraft. Recovery in all cases is straight forward and easy using standard techniques. Maximum height loss in normal recovery from power off stall is 200 ft.

> **CAUTION:** *The stall warning horn is disabled when the flaps are UP.*

### 4.4.17 Spinning

**One and a half turns only are permitted** with aircraft in fully acrobatic configuration, ie. maximum 816 kg AUW.

**Recommended Entry Technique** — With the aircraft trimmed, power off, flaps up, gradually raise the nose above the horizon with wings level. At the point of stall, apply full rudder in direction of the desired spin, at the same time holding the control column centrally hard back. The aircraft will perform an unstable spin with the associated buffet and speed increase. Rate of rotation will be relatively high with a steep nose down attitude. Some aircraft may tend to recover on their own after one full turn.

**Recovery** — Use standard recovery technique. Recovery is almost immediate.

> **CAUTION:** *Excessive 'g' loadings and airspeed build up can occur if recovery is delayed beyond two turns.*

**Inadvertent Spin** — In the event of an inadvertent spin, ensure flaps up, throttle closed. Apply standard recovery technique. Recovery is almost immediate.

### 4.4.18 Diving

Before commencing a dive, the mixture control should be in the fully rich position. With the increase in speed the aircraft can be easily held in the dive to the limiting speed without re-trimming. Directional trim can be maintained by progressive application of rudder.

### 4.4.19 Acrobatics

All normal acrobatic manoeuvres may be performed up to the maximum all up weight of 816 kg. Recommended entry speeds:

| Manoeuvre | Speed |
|---|---|
| Roll | 100 KIAS |
| Barrel Roll | 110 KIAS |
| Stall Turn | 100 KIAS |
| Loop | 125 KIAS |
| Half Roll Off Top of Loop | 140 KIAS |
| Half Roll & Pull Through | 70 KIAS |
| Flick Roll | 70 KIAS |

> **NOTE:** *Owing to possible loss of oil during acrobatics, the engine oil level should be checked as full prior to any flight involving acrobatics.*

> **CAUTION:** *Loss of engine oil pressure during zero and negative 'g' manoeuvres may cause an engine overspeed due to loss of oil pressure to the propeller. If overspeed occurs reduce power (with throttle) immediately, note RPM reached, and have the aircraft inspected by a licensed aircraft engineer before further flight.*

### 4.4.20 Approach & Landing

**Glide Approach** — On the base leg close the throttle and reduce the speed to 70 KIAS and lower flaps (as required). Maintain 70 KIAS throughout the approach, aiming to cross the threshold at 70 KIAS.

**Powered Approach** — Reduce power and airspeed to approximately 1400 RPM and 70 KIAS and select flap as required. Adjust airspeed to 70 KIAS aiming to cross the threshold at 70 KIAS.

> **CAUTION:** *For all approaches select carburettor heat to HOT prior to reducing power.*

**Short Field Landing** — Proceed as for a powered approach, reducing speed to 70 KIAS by the 50 foot height on late final with power off.

> **CAUTION:** *Care must be taken to ensure airspeed is maintained during the landing approach as the aircraft has high drag at approach speed and has a marked tendency to lose airspeed below 70 KIAS.*

**Flapless Landing** — Proceed as for a powered approach but maintain 75 KIAS throughout the approach.

> **CAUTION:** *During both short field and flapless landings care must be taken to avoid the rear tail skid from scraping the ground during the landing flare.*

**Cross Wind Landing** — Initially as for a normal crosswind approach and landing. When landing in a strong cross wind use a wing low, crab, or a combination method of drift correction. The aircraft is easy to handle in all cross wind conditions up to the maximum approved limit.

### 4.4.21 Going Around/Baulked Landing

Open the throttle fully and smoothly. Ensure carburettor heat has been selected to COLD. At full throttle the aircraft will climb away easily with the flaps fully down. Re-trim as necessary. Initially climb at 65 KIAS with the flaps lowered. At a safe height of around 200 feet, raise the flaps in easy stages. There is little change in trim and no sink. Provided obstacle clearance is not critical allow the airspeed to build up to a climb speed of 85 KIAS when the flaps are fully up.

## 6.3 Calculating Actual Weight and Balance

1. Obtain the Empty Weight data for the aircraft from the Load Data Sheet (LDS) and enter data in the table. *(Example: Empty Weight 612 kg, Arm 2054 mm, Moment 1256690 kg mm)*
2. Enter weights of occupants, fuel and baggage in the table.
3. Multiply weights by the corresponding arms to give the moments.
4. Total up the weights and moments. Divide total moment by total weight to give arm: `Arm = Total Moment ÷ Total Weight`
5. Plot Total weight (Take-Off Weight) vs Arm on graph.

The aircraft will be correctly loaded for acrobatic flight provided the plotted point is anywhere within the **acrobatic flight envelope**. The aircraft will be correctly loaded for semi-acrobatic flight provided the plotted point is anywhere within the **semi-acrobatic flight envelope**.

# SECTION 7 — AIRCRAFT & SYSTEMS DESCRIPTIONS

## 7.0 General

There are two variants of the Airtourer T6 model, the T6 and the T6/24. These variants are essentially identical except that the T6/24 has a 24/28 volt electrical system in lieu of the 12/14 volt system fitted to the basic T6 model. Additionally the T6/24 has electrically operated flaps and elevator trim system.

---

## 7.1 Main Services

### 7.1.1 Electrical System

**T6** — Power is supplied by a 40 amp engine driven alternator with its output regulated to a nominal 14 volts. The power is distributed via a DC bus bar at the rear of the electrical panel. A red 'alternator warning light' on the electrical panel will indicate the master switch has been left ON after engine shut-down. The 12 volt, 24 amp hour lead acid battery is contained within a fibreglass box mounted on the left hand side of the rear fuselage, accessible by a hinged panel. The battery will provide ample power to maintain normal services in excess of half an hour after alternator failure. All circuits are protected by circuit breakers on the electrical panel.

**T6/24** — Power is supplied by a 50 amp engine driven alternator with its output regulated to a nominal 28 volts. The 24 volt, 13 amp hour nickel-cadmium battery is contained within a fibreglass box mounted on the left hand side of the rear fuselage, accessible by a hinged panel.

### 7.1.2 Vacuum System

An engine driven vacuum pump provides suction for the Turn & Bank indicator, Artificial Horizon and the Directional Gyro. Suction is controlled by a regulator mounted on the rear face of the firewall behind the instrument panel.

---

## 7.2 Fuel System

### 7.2.1 Fuel Tank
A flexible bag type fuel cell is installed in the wing centre section between the front and rear spar. Total capacity is 131.8 litres, useable capacity is 130.5 litres.

### 7.2.2 Fuel Venting
The tank is vented to the atmosphere from the filling neck via a vent located on the starboard side of the rear fuselage.

### 7.2.3 Fuel Contents Indicating System
An electrically operated fuel contents indicating system provides a continuous indication of the fuel state on the fuel gauge mounted on the fuel panel.

### 7.2.4 Fuel Pumps
An engine driven fuel pump is provided on the engine while an auxiliary electric fuel pump is installed on the firewall. The latter may be selected ON from the cockpit.

### 7.2.5 Fuel Cock
A fuel cock lever marked ON when in fore and aft line of aircraft, and OFF when positioned at right angles to the fore and aft line is situated on the fuel panel adjacent to the base of the control column in the cockpit.

---

## 7.3 Engine Oil System

The oil sump on the engine has a capacity of 8 US quarts (7.6 litres). The oil level is checked by a dip stick embodied in the filler cap accessible by undoing the starboard side engine cowl. A dual oil pressure/temperature gauge is mounted on the instrument panel.

---

## 7.4 Engine Controls

### 7.4.1 Throttle & Mixture Controls
Two throttle controls are provided in throttle quadrants, one on each side of the cockpit, giving each pilot engine control. The mixture control is located on the lower section of the main instrument panel just to the right of the starter button. PULL OUT for LEAN, PUSH IN for RICH.

### 7.4.2 Propeller Pitch Controls
Propeller pitch control levers are provided in the throttle quadrants beside the throttle control levers. Push FORWARD to INCREASE RPM and pull AFT to DECREASE RPM.

### 7.4.3 Carburettor Heat Control
The carburettor heat control is located on the lower section of the main instrument panel just to the left of the cabin heat control. Pull fully out for HOT, push fully in for COLD.

### 7.4.4 Engine Starting Controls

**T6** — The engine starter is engaged by pressing the starter button situated centrally under the instrument panel. ***Do not engage the starter while the propeller is rotating*** otherwise serious damage may result. The ignition switch is key operated and located on the electrical panel. Each magneto may be individually selected but the engine is normally started on LEFT.

**T6/24** — The engine starter is engaged by rotating the combined ignition/starter switch fully clockwise past the spring detent. This position automatically selects LEFT magneto only for the start. ***Do not engage the starter while the propeller is rotating*** otherwise serious damage may result.

---

## 7.5 Aircraft Controls

### 7.5.1 Control Column
The control column is situated forward of the centre armrest and operates the ailerons, flapperons, and the elevator. The elevator is controlled by fore and aft movement of the control column and the ailerons/flapperons by side to side movement.

### 7.5.2 Rudder Pedals
The rudder pedals are of conventional form and are adjustable over four inches. They are rigidly connected to the rudder, giving positive rudder control. Direct connection from the pedals to the nosewheel provides nosewheel steering.

### 7.5.3 Trim Controls

**T6** — Elevator trim is provided by means of the trim lever protruding from the centre armrest. It operates in the natural sense, ie. its down movement trims the aircraft nose down, up trims nose up. To trim first disengage the lever with the left hand by pulling it to port, operate up or down, until there is no stick force on the control column and re-engage by pushing it home (ie. to starboard).

**T6/24** — Elevator trim is provided by an electric actuator controlled by a trim switch mounted on the control column. A trim position indicator is located on the centre lower panel below the ignition/starter switch.

### 7.5.4 Flap Controls

**T6** — The flap lever is situated on the left hand wall of the cockpit and is accessible to the pilot in the port seat. The flap lever operates in the natural sense and has five locking positions:
- **Zero notch:** Flaps fully down, cruising configuration
- **First notch:** 4° of flap — for reduced speed in the circuit and slow cruising
- **Second notch:** 8° of flap — normally used for take-off
- **Third notch:** 12° of flap — for landing in gusty weather conditions
- **Fourth notch (full):** 15° of flap — for landing, provides steepest rate of descent

**T6/24** — The flaps are operated by an electric actuator controlled by two flap selector switches, one on either end of the main instrument panel, providing either pilot with the ability to operate the flaps. A flap position indicator is located on the centre lower panel below the ammeter.

### 7.5.5 Wheel Brakes
The main wheels are fitted with hydraulic disc type brakes. Even pressure for braking is supplied to both brakes when either hand brake lever is pulled. A parking brake selector of 'lift to park' type is centrally mounted and brakes can be locked ON by first pulling the brake handle and then raising the park selector.

---

## 7.6 Flight Instruments

The cockpit is equipped with adequate instruments for purposes of normal flying. The Artificial Horizon and Directional Gyro are operated by suction provided by an engine driven vacuum pump. The Turn and Bank Indicator is electrically powered. The Air Speed Indicator, Vertical Speed Indicator and Altimeter are operated by the pitot static system, comprising a combined pressure and static head on the outboard end under the starboard wing. A magnetic compass is installed centrally on top of the instrument panel.

---

## 7.7 General Equipment

### 7.7.1 Cockpit Canopy
A sliding perspex canopy covers the cockpit. An external handle protruding centrally from the top leading edge of the canopy enables the canopy to be opened by simply turning this through 90° and pulling backwards. The cockpit is entered from each side by climbing on the wing, opening the sliding canopy fully and stepping into the cockpit. The canopy may be opened in flight up to speeds of 70 KIAS and on the ground during taxying. The canopy should be closed during take-off, climb and landing.

### 7.7.2 Seats & Harnesses
Non adjustable seats are fitted and equipped with full restraint harnesses.

### 7.7.3 Maps & Stowage
A stowage space is provided for maps, etc. in the form of flush pockets on the side of the cockpit.

### 7.7.4 Baggage Compartment
A baggage compartment is situated aft of the seat frame with a structural capacity of 45 kg (100 lbs), and a volumetric capacity of 7.5 ft³.

### 7.7.5 Cabin Heating & Ventilation
Cabin heating is provided by warm air from an outlet above the feet at each pilot position. The hot air control is situated centrally under the instrument panel. Pull for cabin heat ON. Demisting of the windscreen is coupled together with cabin heating. To demist the windscreen the cabin heat must be ON. Ventilation is provided through an intake on each side of the cockpit with an individual control at each pilot position.

---

## 7.8 Emergency Equipment

### 7.8.1 Fire Extinguisher
When fitted fire extinguishers are usually mounted on quick release brackets in front of the seats.

### 7.8.2 First Aid Kits
Carriage of first aid kits is not specifically provided for, however when carried these can be carried in the baggage compartment.

### 7.8.3 Emergency Exits
When necessary the sliding canopy can be opened rapidly in flight at speeds up to 70 KIAS by unlocking and pulling backwards on the handle in the normal manner.

## 8.5 Ground Handling

The aircraft can be easily manoeuvred by hand on the ground by having one man pulling or pushing as required using the tow bar attached to the nose wheel. When required additional people can assist by pushing on the wing (not the control surfaces).

**Handgrips, Walkways & Steps** — A handgrip is provided on each side of the cockpit just below the sliding canopy. A walkway coated with a black anti-slip material extends over the wings on both sides of the cockpit. It is not permissible to stand or walk on any other part of the wing. ***Do Not Stand on the Flap!***

---

## 8.6 Parking

When possible park the aircraft into the anticipated wind and set the parking brakes. Do not set the parking brakes during cold weather when accumulated moisture may freeze the brakes, or when the brakes are very hot. Lock the elevator and aileron controls using the control lock. The rudder may be locked using a suitable locally manufactured external rudder lock between the rudder balance horn and the fin. Install wheel chocks when available. When severe weather conditions or high winds are anticipated, the best precaution is to hangar the aircraft.

---

## 8.7 Tying Down

1. Park the aircraft into wind when practical.
2. Lock the ailerons and elevator (and rudder if possible) as described above.
3. Chock the wheels.
4. Attach a strong (recommended at least 400 kg tensile strength) tie down rope to the wing tie-down lugs, and the tail lug at approximately 45° to the ground. Leave some slack to avoid damage due to rope shrinkage that sometimes occurs when a moist rope dries out.

---

## 8.8 Jacking

The aircraft may be jacked in order to service the landing gear, change wheels/tyres and to perform other minor service functions:

1. Place the two main jacks under the wing surface aft of the main undercarriage legs. Use suitable jacking adaptors so that the jacks are securely engaged in the jacking point holes.
2. Place a third jack under the jacking point located on the lower firewall just to the right of the nose undercarriage leg.
3. Raise the jacks until the wheels are clear of the ground as required keeping the aircraft relatively level during the jacking process.

**Notes:**
1. *If only one wheel needs to be raised clear of the ground such as for changing a wheel or tyre, a single jack may be used. Ensure the aircraft is securely chocked to prevent movement when jacked.*
2. *If only one main wheel needs to be raised clear of the ground and a suitable jacking adaptor is available to attach to the main undercarriage leg, a small bottle jack or similar may be used.*

---

## 8.9 Levelling

The aircraft is longitudinally level when an accurate level placed along an upper longeron in the cockpit area gives a level indication. The aircraft is laterally level when an accurate level placed across the cockpit between the two upper longerons gives a level indication.

## 8.10 Fluid Servicing

### 8.10.1 Fuel System

**Filling Fuel Tanks** — Observe all the required precautions for handling fuel and filling tanks. Ensure that the aircraft is bonded to EARTH using the earthing point on the nose leg.

**Fuel Draining/Sampling** — A quick drain fuel drain fitting is provided in the fuel tank sump under the centre fuselage. Fuel should be drained/sampled from this point before the first flight of the day and after each subsequent refuelling.

### 8.10.2 Engine Lubrication System

**Filling the Engine Sump** — The engine sump should be filled to the operating level with the lubricating oil specified in para 2.4.3. Open the cowl access door to gain access to the oil dipstick/filler tube.

**Draining the Sump** — The engine sump is drained by removing the lower engine cowl and removing the sump drain plug. Ensure that the sump drain plug is correctly replaced and locked prior to refilling the engine with oil.

---

## 8.11 Wheel Brake Fluid

The brake fluid level may be checked by opening the left hand engine cowl and removing the master cylinder reservoir cap. If necessary it may be topped up with mineral based hydraulic fluid as specified in the Airtourer Maintenance and Repair Manual. *Ensure that no contaminants are allowed to enter the reservoir, particularly when removing or replacing the cap.*

## 8.12 Undercarriage

The main undercarriage is a simple spring steel leg and apart from normal inspections for security, is maintenance free between scheduled maintenance inspections. The nose undercarriage leg utilises a coil spring as the shock absorbing medium. This spring is damped using a automotive type hydraulic damper. The spring requires no maintenance. The hydraulic damper requires no maintenance in the field.

## 8.14 Induction Air Filter

Dust and dirt must be prevented from entering the engine induction system. Dust and dirt ingested into the engine is probably the greatest single cause of premature engine wear. When operating in a very dusty environment the filter may need to be cleaned more regularly than the time periods specified in the Airtourer Maintenance and Repair Manual.

## 8.15 Battery Service

**T6** — The battery is a conventional lead acid aircraft battery. Servicing involves adding distilled water to maintain the electrolyte level, checking and cleaning the terminals, and checking the battery's security. Spilled electrolyte or corrosion may be neutralised and cleaned off using bicarbonate of soda (baking soda) and clean water.

**T6/24** — The battery is a nickel cadmium aircraft battery. Maintenance on this battery type must be carried out by suitably trained maintenance personnel only. ***No pilot maintenance is permitted.***

## 8.16 Flyable Storage

Aircraft in storage for a maximum of 30 days or those which receive only intermittent operational use for the first 50 hours are considered to be in flyable storage status. If the aircraft is not flown the propeller should be rotated by hand through at least six revolutions every seventh day. Stop the propeller 45°–90° from the original position.

> **WARNING:** *For maximum safety, check that both magnetos are OFF, the throttle closed, the mixture control is in the ICO position, and the aircraft is secured before rotating the propeller by hand. Do not stand within the arc of the propeller blades while turning the propeller.*

After 30 days, the aircraft should be flown for at least 30 minutes. If the aircraft is to be out of service for a period longer than 30 days the engine should be inhibited in accordance with the engine manufacturers instructions.

> **NOTE:** *The best method of preventing corrosion of the cylinders and other internal parts of the engine is to fly the aircraft at least once a week for long enough to reach normal operating temperatures. This will vaporise moisture and other by-products of combustion.*

## 8.17 Cleaning and Similar Care

### 8.17.1 Windshield & Canopy

The windshield and canopy are made from a plastic perspex material. The following procedure is recommended:

1. Flush with clean water and dislodge excess dirt, mud, etc. using your bare hand so that you can feel any hard to remove lumps and be able to gently remove them.
2. Wash with a mild soap and warm water. Use a soft cloth or sponge. Do not rub excessively.
3. Rinse thoroughly, then dry with a clean moist chamois. Do not rub with a dry cloth as this builds up an electrostatic charge which attracts dust. Oil and grease may be removed by rubbing lightly with a soft cloth moistened with kerosene. *Do not use volatile solvents such as gasoline, alcohol, benzine, carbon tetrachloride, lacquer thinner or most commercial window cleaning sprays, as they will soften and craze the plastic.*
4. After washing, the windshield and canopy should be cleaned using an aircraft windshield cleaner following carefully the manufacturers instructions.

### 8.17.2 Painted Surfaces

The painted exterior surfaces of the aircraft can be washed using a mild detergent and water. Special aircraft cleaning detergents may be used or alternatively an automotive liquid detergent provided it is non corrosive and contains no abrasive materials. Stubborn oil and grease may be removed using a small amount of solvent such as kerosene.

### 8.17.3 Propeller Care

Preflight inspection of propeller blades for nicks, and wiping them occasionally with an oily cloth to clean off grass and bug stains will ensure long and trouble free service. Small nicks on the propeller, particularly near the tips and on the rear face near the leading edge and on the leading edge itself, should be dressed out as soon as possible as these nicks produce stress concentrations, and if ignored, may result in cracks and subsequent blade failure. Never use an alkaline cleaner on the blades; remove grease and oil with a suitable solvent such as kerosene.

### 8.17.4 Engine Compartment

The engine compartment should be kept clean to minimise any danger of fire, and to allow proper inspection of engine components. The engine and engine compartment may be washed down with a suitable solvent, then dried thoroughly.

> **CAUTION:** *Particular care should be given to electrical equipment before cleaning. Solvent should not be allowed to enter magnetos, starter, generator/alternator, and the like. These components should be protected before saturating the engine with solvent. Any oil, fuel, and air openings on the engine and accessories should be covered before washing the engine with solvent. Caustic cleaning solutions should be used cautiously and should always be properly neutralised after their use.*
