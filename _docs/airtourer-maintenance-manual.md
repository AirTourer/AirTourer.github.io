## FOREWORD

Correct maintenance, repair and overhaul of a light aircraft ensures less time in the repair and overhaul shop, lengthens the aircraft life and renders satisfaction to the owner. Ease of maintenance has been a major consideration in the designing of the Airtourer.

The information presented within is divided into three sections: **Maintenance**, **Repair** and **Overhaul**.

The **Repair Section** contains instructions and descriptive information to enable permissible repairs to the aircraft structure to be carried out in the field. The decision of whether to repair or replace, in the case of a major repair, will be influenced by a comparison of the relative cost and time factors. The manufacturers should be consulted if there is any doubt about a repair not specifically mentioned within the repair section.

The **Maintenance Section** provides sufficient information to enable all normal servicing, inspection, trouble shooting, adjustments, removals and replacements to be carried out by an engineer in the field.

The **Overhaul Section** contains specific instructions to enable work to be carried out on units and assemblies removed from the aircraft, and is therefore primarily for the use of the approved repair organisation.

Amendment sheets will be issued as necessary and should be incorporated in the manual. Service Bulletins will also be issued from time to time to notify the owner of modifications, requirements and recommendations.

The **Airtourer Spare Parts Manual** is a supplementary publication to this manual.

# CHAPTER 2 — LIFTING AND SHORING

## 1. General
When lifting the aircraft, **Slinging Beam Pt. No. 05009** and **Sling Pt. No. 05008** should be used in conjunction with the upper engine mount attachments and the rear fuselage adjacent to the fuselage steps.

## 2. Lifting Beam
The lifting beam consists of two back-to-back aluminium alloy extrusions. The fulcrum point can be varied to suit the aircraft state:
- Less wing
- Fully equipped
- With full fuel
- Less engine
- Less engine and wing

## 3. Sling
The rear sling is a 1″ wide terylene strap capable of taking a load of 1,500 lbs. Pass the strap under the rear fuselage steps; two loops attach to the fuselage steps to prevent rearward sliding. The forward sling is a flexible wire rope assembly passed around the upper engine mount attachment clusters.

> **NOTE:** Fit the rear sling first. Ensure the forward part of the sling is hard up against the firewall, otherwise bending of the engine mount may result.

## 4. Lifting the Engine
Use engine sling **Pt. No. 05013/1** in conjunction with the lifting eye on the engine crankcase.

## 5. Raising the Nose Wheel
On aircraft Serial No. 66 and subsequent, a jacking mount is located at the bottom of the forward firewall face. For aircraft prior to Serial 66, depress the tailplane and secure with a rope through the fuselage tie-down eye.

> **CAUTION: DO NOT LIFT THE NOSE BY MEANS OF THE ENGINE LIFTING EYE.**

## 6. Jacking
Jacking mounts are situated on the under surface of the wing, aft of the main undercarriage legs. Remove the plug from the jacking mount, lift the aircraft and place a padded trestle under the fuselage behind the firewall.

# CHAPTER 4 — TOWING AND TAXYING

## 1. Towing and Pushing
Insert tow bar **02-05006** into the hollow axle of the nosewheel. With the propeller horizontal, grasp it at the root to push or pull.

> **CAUTION:** Do not push or pull at the outboard end of the propeller. Nosewheel travel is limited to approximately 12½° port and 16½° starboard.

## 2. Taxying
Release the parking brake and use minimum power to start moving (~1,200 RPM). During taxi and especially downwind, hold RPM down to prevent excessive speed. Use brakes sparingly.

> **CAUTION:** Care should be exercised when taxying over rough fields. Taxying over loose gravel should be done at approximately 1,000 RPM.

# CHAPTER 5 — PARKING AND MOORING

## 1. Parking
**To apply parking brakes:**
1. Pull the brake handle fully back
2. While holding pressure, pull out the parking brake knob on the centre console
3. Release handle

**To release:** Pull brake handle.

## 2. Covers
Cockpit enclosure and engine covers should be kept in position when parked in the open.

## 3. Mooring
Three mooring eyes are provided: one at each wing tip and one at the rear of the fuselage. Use dog clips to attach mooring ropes.

**Gust lock installation:**
1. Fit the metal bracket over the top of the control column
2. Loop the cable ends over the clips on the top longeron forward of the windscreen arch

# CHAPTER 7 — SERVICING

## 1. Refuelling
Aircraft is serviced with **80 octane minimum**. Tank capacity: 29 gallons (including 0.3 gallon unusable).

1. Ensure purity of fuel
2. Bond fuel filling hose to aircraft before refuelling
3. Do not overfill
4. Drain sufficient fuel from the tank drain to clear water

## 2. Lubrication

### Airtourer 100
**Continental Specification MHS24**

| Ambient Temp. | Grade |
|---|---|
| Below 40°F | Aviation Grade 1040 – SAE 20 |
| Above 40°F | Aviation Grade 1080 – SAE 40 |

### Airtourer 115
**Lycoming Specification 30-1-E**

| Condition | Straight Mineral | Multi-Grade Additive | Oil Inlet Temp Desired | Max |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Above 60°F | SAE 50 | SAE 40 or 50 | 82°C | 118°C |
| 30°–90°F | SAE 40 | SAE 40 | 82°C | 118°C |
| 0°–70°F | SAE 30 | SAE 40 | 76°C | 107°C |

## 3. Filling Oil Sump
Service with specified grade oil. Normal sump capacity: **5 Imp. quarts (6 U.S. quarts)**.

## 4. Cleaning

- **External surfaces:** Detergent wash, then high-grade automotive polish
- **Painted surfaces:** Mild soap and water, then automotive wax
- **Engine compartment:** Suitable solvent, then dry thoroughly
- **Upholstery:** Vacuum cleaner; foam detergent for stains; avoid saturating with volatile solvent
- **Propeller:** Wipe occasionally with an oily rag
- **Windshield/Canopy:** Household detergent and warm water; never dry-wipe

## 5. Topping Up Nose Leg Strut
With nose leg depressed until steering arm is 4¼″ below the upper bearing, top up with approved shock absorber oil until excess spills from filler hole. Bounce and refill until level remains constant.

## 6. Topping Up Brake System
Unscrew cap on master brake cylinder and top up with specified hydraulic fluid.

> **CAUTION:** Do not mix different specifications of hydraulic fluid.

## 7. Bleeding the Brake System
The brake system must be bled whenever a brake line has been disconnected. Use a hydraulic hand pump with correct grade of brake fluid. Continue pumping until fluid flowing into the jar is free of air bubbles.

> **CAUTION:** Do not replenish with fluid drained from the system.

## 8. Fuel Filter Check & Renewal
Check every **25 hours**. Unscrew the central bolt, lower the element canister and bowl, clean and renew element if clogged.

## 9. Topping Up the Battery
Check every **week or 25 hours** (whichever comes first). Top up with distilled water to the level of the perforated plate.

## 10. Bleeding the Fuel System
Bleed whenever a fuel line has been disconnected. With fuel in tank, Master Switch ON, electric fuel pump ON — loosen the inlet hose at the carburettor end until fuel flow is free of air bubbles.

## 11. Cleaning the Air Filter
Clean every **100 hours** or more often in dirty conditions. Wash in clean unleaded petrol, blow from inside to outside, then spray or brush a light coating of engine oil on front and rear element surfaces.

> **CAUTION: DO NOT OVERTIGHTEN FILTER LOCKING STUDS.**

# CHAPTER 8 — INSPECTION

## 1. Daily Inspection
Carried out prior to the first flight of each day.

## 2. Between Flight Inspection
1. Inspect wings, fuselage, empennage and control surfaces for security and obvious damage
2. Ensure windshield and canopy are clean
3. Fuel tank serviced and filler cap secure; fuel vent clear
4. Visually check underside of fuselage for fuel leaks

## 3. 25 Hourly Inspection
*(Every 25 hours or once a month, whichever comes first)*

1. Check filter bowl for water and sediment
2. Inspect aileron differential control boxes
3. Check battery level and top up if necessary
4. Drain sump and refill with specified engine oil
5. Clean main fuel filter
6. Lubricate aileron and flapperon pivot joints

## 4. 100 Hourly Inspection
*(Every 100 flying hours — includes all daily inspection requirements)*

### Airframe
1. Check flight and serviceability log
2. Clean aircraft internally and externally
3. Inspect undercarriage legs, wheels, tyres, nose wheel strut
   - Check tyre inflation: **Main 23 psi / Nose 12 psi (AT100) or 16 psi (AT115)**
4. Inspect cabin interior, seats, safety belts, canopy and windscreen
5. Inspect fuselage skin for damage, dents, loose rivets, corrosion
6. Inspect empennage (tailplane, elevators, fin, rudder)
7. Inspect wing skin, ailerons, flapperons, centre flap, gap tapes
8. Inspect all flying controls for full and free travel
9. Lubricate per lubrication chart

### Instruments
1. Check flight and serviceability log
2. Inspect instruments for security, condition and legibility
3. Check compass needle friction; altimeter; gyro caging devices
4. Drain pitot-static watertraps; check orifices; carry out leak test
5. Check venturi/vacuum system

### Electrical
1. Check battery electrolyte level and specific gravity; inspect battery box for corrosion
2. Inspect starter, generator, commutators, brush holders
3. Check all switches, circuit breakers, generator warning light
4. Check stall warning system function

### Engine
1. Clean engine; drain oil; remove and inspect oil filters for metal particles
2. Inspect engine mount bolts and rubbers
3. Inspect cylinders, valve gear, rocker covers
4. Remove spark plugs; check and clean; reset gaps:
   - **Continental O-200-A:** 0.015″–0.018″
   - **Lycoming O-235-C1:** 0.025″ (unshielded), 0.015″ (shielded)
5. Check cylinder compressions
6. Inspect magnetos and ignition wiring; check point gaps (0.012″–0.024″)
7. Inspect cylinder baffles
8. Inspect fuel system plumbing, filters, tank vent
9. Inspect and service carburettor, hot air system, air filter
10. Inspect induction and exhaust manifolds, heat exchange shrouds
11. Inspect engine controls (throttle, mixture, carb heat)
12. Inspect cowlings and fasteners
13. Inspect propeller and spinner; check track within **0.125″**
14. Test run engine per Chapter 9

## 5. Special Inspections

### Heavy or Overweight Landing
1. Inspect complete airframe externally for visible damage (sheared rivets, buckled skin)
2. Inspect mainplane-to-fuselage attachments
3. Inspect empennage structure
4. Inspect engine mount attachments and adjacent structure
5. Remove undercarriage fairings; inspect leg attachments (magnetic particle inspection if doubt exists)
6. Inspect nose leg strut bearings and mounting structure
7. Inspect wheels, axles, bearings and brake mechanisms
8. Functional test of instruments and radio; check battery

### Severe Turbulence
1. Inspect complete airframe externally
2. Inspect mainplane-to-fuselage and empennage attachments
3. Check engine mountings
4. Inspect control surfaces, hinge points, cables and bellcranks
5. Check seats, seatbelts, instruments and equipment

### Lightning Strike
1. Detailed external inspection for burning, scorching and pitting (especially aerials, wing extremities)
2. Check control surface hinge points and bonding
3. Check all internal bonding; inspect fuel lines and vents
4. Functional check of all electrical, instrument and radio services
5. Carry out compass swing

### Propeller Damage (Ground/Obstruction Contact)
1. Remove propeller for inspection by approved organisation
2. Check engine crankshaft run-out at propeller flange — **maximum 0.005″ (full indicator reading)**
3. Inspect crankcase in vicinity of engine mounting feet and crankshaft thrust bearing housing
4. Inspect engine mount frames for bowing, weld condition, and attachment bolts for shear

# CHAPTER 9 — GROUND RUN-UP PROCEDURE

## Continental O-200-A Engine (Airtourer 100)

### Pre-Starting
- Ignition switch: **OFF**
- Mixture: **FULL LEAN**
- Turn propeller over by hand 4 revolutions, noting compressions and impulse operations

### Starting Procedure
| Item | Action |
|---|---|
| Fuel | ON |
| Master Switch | ON |
| Electric Pump | ON |
| Generator Switch | ON |
| Prime | Two strokes of throttle |
| Throttle | Slightly open (~½″ forward) |
| Mixture | FULL RICH |
| Carburettor Heat | COLD |
| Ignition Switch | BOTH ON |
| Starter | Establish "All Clear" then PULL |

> **CAUTION:** Limit starter operation to 10 seconds at 2-minute intervals. If no oil pressure within 30 seconds, stop and investigate.

### Warm Up
Idle at **800 RPM** for approximately 3 minutes (or until oil temperature minimum 24°C). Check magneto switches for dead cut. Check carburettor heat operation.

### Run-Up
Increase smoothly to **1,700 RPM**. Check magneto drop — maximum **100 RPM** on either magneto; maximum difference **50 RPM**.

### Operating Limits

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Oil Pressure (normal) | 30–60 PSI |
| Oil Pressure (idling min.) | 10 PSI |
| Cylinder Head Temp. max. | 274°C (525°F) |
| Oil Temperature max. | 107°C (225°F) |
| Fuel Pressure | 2–4 PSI |

### Engine Stop
Run at 800–1,000 RPM to cool. Then: Mixture → FULL LEAN. After engine stops: Ignition OFF, Throttle CLOSED, Electric Pump OFF, Generator OFF, Master Switch OFF.

# CHAPTER 11 — ELECTRICAL SYSTEMS

## 1. Electric Power Supply System
12 volt DC, single wire earth return system comprising: battery, generator, voltage/current regulator, starter, battery master relay, and electrical panel with generator warning light, circuit breakers and switches.

## 2. Battery
12 volt, 24 ampere-hour capacity, mounted in battery box on the firewall. Isolated by battery relay controlled by master switch.

## 3. To Remove Battery
1. Remove two 4BA screws locking battery cradle
2. Slide battery and cradle from box
3. Disconnect **earth (negative) cable first**
4. Disconnect positive cable
5. Raise forward end of cradle over stop and withdraw

> **CAUTION: Always remove the earth cable first and replace it last.**

## 5. Battery Maintenance — Hydrometer Readings

| Specific Gravity | State of Charge |
|---|---|
| 1.280 | 100% charged |
| 1.250 | 75% charged |
| 1.220 | 50% charged |
| 1.190 | 25% charged |
| 1.160 | Practically dead |

*(Readings based on electrolyte temperature of 80°F)*

## 6. Battery Fault/Remedy

| Fault | Probable Cause | Remedy |
|---|---|---|
| No power to bus when master on | Dead battery | Replace or charge battery |
| No power to bus when master on | Defective master switch or wiring | Repair wiring / replace relay |
| No power to bus when master on | Faulty battery cable | Replace cable; clean and reconnect |
| Supplies power but won't turn engine | Low battery | Charge battery |
| Supplies power but won't turn engine | Faulty battery cables | Clean and reconnect |
| Supplies power but won't turn engine | Cell shorting under load | Replace battery |
| Supplies power but won't turn engine | Defective starter | Repair wiring / replace starter |
| Excessive water usage | Charging rate too high | Adjust or replace regulator |

## 8. Battery Box Maintenance
Clean periodically with strong bicarbonate of soda solution. Remove hard deposits with wire brush. Flush with clean water and check drain tube is clear.

> **WARNING:** Do not allow acid deposits to contact skin or clothing. Wash immediately with soap and water.

## 11. Generator Power System
20 ampere generator, voltage regulator, generator field switch, isolation circuit breaker and warning light. Charging rate should be 2–5 amps with normal electrical load.

## 22. Stall Warning
Audible warning circuit: lift detector switch in leading edge of port wing, wired to warning horn under luggage compartment floor. Activates when stalling is imminent with flaps down.

# CHAPTER 13 — FLIGHT CONTROLS

## 1. General
The flight control system consists of aileron/flapperon, flap, rudder, elevator and elevator trim control systems. All can be operated from either side except the flap control (port side only).

## 3. Adjustment of Aileron and Flapperon Controls
1. Set control stick vertical (aircraft in rigging position)
2. Adjust flapperon push-pull rods until flapperons are in neutral position
3. Check ailerons are in neutral
4. Adjust aileron push-pull rod in differential box if needed (yellow band identifies pinned end)
5. Set flapperon/aileron stops: **23° to port and 23° to starboard**

**Angular movement checks (stick held 23° to port):**

| Surface | Movement |
|---|---|
| Port flapperon | 7° 54′ up |
| Starboard flapperon | 4° 25′ down |
| Port aileron | 8° 10′ up |
| Starboard aileron | 3° 3′ down |

### Flapperon Neutral Setting
The flapperons are in neutral when the trailing edge centreline is **22.5″ below the fuselage datum line**.

## 10. Rudder Control System
Stirrup-type rudder pedals connected via push-pull rod to idler lever, with pre-stretched cables to the rudder. Rudder movement limited by a stop at the base of the fin.

### Adjustment
1. Set port pedals in rearmost notch; adjust push-pull rod until idler lever is square with aircraft centreline
2. Adjust cable turnbuckles to centre the rudder (horn coincides with top of fin)
3. Tension cables to **50 lbs**

## 14. Elevator Control System
Control column linked to elevator via two push-pull rods joined at rear of cockpit by idler lever.

**Control limits:**
- AT100: 12½° forward; 14° 12′ aft
- AT115: 12½° forward; 18° 23′ aft

**Elevator travel:**
- AT100: 19° up / 15° down
- AT115: 22° up / 15° down

# CHAPTER 14 — FUEL SYSTEM

## 1. General
Fuel drawn from tank via fuel cock → filter → tee fitting → electric pump or engine driven pump → carburettor.

## 12. Fuel Tank
Rubber cell type, contained between inboard wing ribs. Usable capacity: **28.7 Imperial gallons**; total capacity: **29 Imperial gallons**.

## 18. Fuel System Trouble Chart

| Fault | Probable Cause | Check & Remedy |
|---|---|---|

| No fuel in carburettor | Fuel line disconnected | Inspect and reconnect |
| No fuel in carburettor | Fuel tank empty | Service with correct grade |
| No fuel in carburettor | Carburettor filter blocked | Inspect and clean |
| No fuel in carburettor | Electric pump faulty | Check pump operation |

# CHAPTER 16 — LANDING GEAR

## 1. General
Main landing gear: two tapered spring steel cantilever legs clamped and bolted to secondary structure in wing. 5″ diameter Dunlop wheel assemblies with hydraulic disc brakes.

## 3. Installation of Main Landing Gear — Torque Values
- Clamp plate bolts (AN6-H16A): **196–240 in·lbs**
- Inner attachment bolt (Pt. No. 61033/1): **456–504 in·lbs**

# CHAPTER 50 — GENERAL REPAIR PROCEDURES

## 2. Basic Rules for Repair

- **Assessment:** Examine surrounding parts methodically; check bolt/rivet holes for elongation; perform alignment checks where major damage occurs
- **Support:** Support structure on suitable trestles before commencing repairs
- **Strength:** Use materials of quality and specification at least equal to original; use same type and size of rivet

### Preparation
- **Dents:** Dress with fibre mallet and wooden blocks; examine for cracks
- **Nicks:** Clean to smooth-edged curvature
- **Tears:** Dress to regular shape; remove ragged edges
- **Scores:** Negligible if depth ≤10% of plate thickness and length <15% of panel width
- **Cracks:** Drill No. 40 (0.098″) hole at each extremity to prevent spreading
- **Corrosion:** Clean out and treat with Alodine if within score/scratch limits

## 3. Skin Panel Damage Assessment

| Damage Type | Location | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| Shallow dent (no cracks/creases) | Less critical | Negligible if ≤0.2″ deep, <5″ diameter (limit: one per panel) |
| Shallow dent (no cracks/creases) | High critical | Negligible if ≤0.06″ deep, <2″ diameter |
| Sharp dent | All panels | Replace damaged area |
| Dent with crease | All panels | Replace damaged area |
| Holes | Less critical | Patch if ≤1″ diameter |
| Holes | Semi-critical | Patch if ≤¾″ diameter |
| Cracks up to 15% panel width | Less critical | Drill 1/8″ hole at crack end and patch |
| Cracks | Semi/high critical | Replace cracked area |

# CHAPTER 51 — STANDARD REPAIRS

## 2. Fibre Glass Repairs
Use any commercial polyester resin. For cracks: apply resin and one/two layers of glass cloth inside as a doubler. For holes: trim damaged area, apply glass mat over hole inside, cover outside with cellophane, apply epoxy resin and sand smooth after curing.

## 4. Repairs to Acrylic Canopy

- **Cracks:** Drill 1/8″ holes at each end; scrape to "V" shape; fill with acrylic cement
- **Holes:** Clean to circular shape; bevel edges to 45°; fit acrylic patch of same thickness; secure with acrylic cement
- **Scratches:** Sand with No. 320 (or finer) wet sandpaper using circular motion; buff with motor-driven pad at max. ~2,000 ft/min to restore transparency

> **NOTE:** Repair only where pilot's field of view is not affected. Damaged areas affecting vision require panel renewal.

> **CAUTION: Never use coarser than No. 320 abrasive.**

## 7. Repairs to Welded Joints
Use low carbon content welding rod. Grind or file joint to "V" profile for maximum penetration.

**Welding rod composition:**

| Element | Limit |
|---|---|
| Carbon | 0.10% max |
| Manganese | 0.30% |
| Phosphorous | 0.04% max |
| Sulphur | 0.04% max |
